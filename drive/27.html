question:Maximum-likelihood parameter estimation in terahertz time-domain spectroscopy Laleh Mohtashemi, Paul Westlund, Derek G. Sahota, Graham B. Lea, Ian Bushfield, Payam Mousavi, and J. Steven Dodge Introduction At the heart of most applications of terahertz time-domain spectroscopy (THz-TDS) is a mathematical procedure that relates raw THz-TDS waveform measurements to parameters of scientific and technological interest. Typically this analysis is formulated in the frequency domain, since it provides the most natural description of any linear, time-invariant system of interest. But since a THz-TDS waveform describes the electric field as a function of time, it must be Fourier-transformed for use in any frequency-domain analysis. The Fourier transform scrambles the THz-TDS measurement noise, which is more naturally represented in the time domain, and produces artifacts that can degrade the overall measurement quality and yield misleading results. Furthermore, the standard approaches to parameter estimation in THz-TDS involve the ratio of two noisy waveforms, which further distorts the noise spectrum and can yield noise distributions that are far from Gaussian. Other approaches have emerged that improve on the standard procedures, but so far all of the existing approaches to THz-TDS analysis lack clear, model-independent statistical criteria for establishing parameter confidence intervals or for deciding whether a given physical model describes the data well or not. Here, we introduce a general framework to remedy this. We describe a maximum-likelihood approach to THz-TDS analysis in which both the signal and the noise are treated explicitly in the time domain, together with a frequency-domain constraint between the input and the output signal. We show that this approach produces superior results to existing analysis methods. Signal and noise in THz-TDS A Monte Carlo simulation of an elementary THz-TDS analysis illustrates the basic problems that our method solves; it also allows us to develop notational conventions that we will use to describe our main results. Transfer function estimation in THz-TDS To measure the response of a system requires measurements of two THz-TDS waveforms: an input, mu(t), and an output, psi(t) = [hensuremath{*}mu](t), where h(t) is the impulse response of the system and the ensuremath{*} symbol denotes continuous-time convolution. Fourier transforming the ideal relationship psi(t) = [hensuremath{*}mu](t) converts the time-domain convolution to a frequency-domain multiplication, tilde{psi}_text{c}(omega) = H(omega)tilde{mu}_text{c}(omega), where tilde{mu}_text{c}(omega) and tilde{psi}_text{c}(omega) denote the continuous-time Fourier transforms of mu(t) and psi(t), respectively, and H(omega) = tilde{h}_text{c}(omega) is the continuous-time transfer function of the system. Proceeding as we did for a single waveform, we simulate input and output waveform measurements x(t_k) and y(t_k), respectively, by computing mu(t) and psi(t) at the discrete times t_k and adding time-domain noise sigma_mu(t_k)epsilon(t_{k}) and sigma_psi(t_k)delta(t_k), where each epsilon(t_k) and delta(t_k) is an independent random variable with a standard normal distribution. After applying the discrete Fourier transform to obtain X(omega_l) = tilde{x}(omega_l) and Y(omega_l) = tilde{y}(omega_l), we can determine the empirical transfer function estimate (ETFE), hat{H}_text{ETFE}(omega_l) = frac{Y(omega_l)}{X(omega_l)} = frac{tilde{psi}(omega_l) + [tilde{sigma}_psiensuremath{circledast}tilde{epsilon}](omega_l)}{tilde{mu}(omega_l) + [tilde{sigma}_muensuremath{circledast}tilde{delta}](omega_l)}, label{eq:ETFE} where tilde{mu}(omega_l) and tilde{psi}(omega_l) are the DFTs of the ideal input and output signals, respectively, that we would obtain in the absence of noise, and [tilde{sigma}_muensuremath{circledast}tilde{epsilon}](omega_l) and [tilde{sigma}_psiensuremath{circledast}tilde{delta}](omega_l) are the corresponding frequency-domain noise terms. The ETFE is a common starting point for THz-TDS analysis—it is easy to compute from the raw data, and it provides an estimate of H(omega) for any linear, time-invariant system. Frequently, though, the focus of interest is not H(omega) itself but a relatively small number of parameters that specify H(omega) through a physical model, such as the thickness and refractive index of a material. In this case, fitting the model directly to hat{H}_text{ETFE}(omega_l) can yield ambiguous results, because hat{H}_text{ETFE}(omega_l) is biased and has noise that is both correlated and non-Gaussian. To illustrate these deficiencies, Fig. 2 shows 250 simulations of hat{H}_text{ETFE}(omega_l) with nominally identical input and output pulses, corresponding to H(omega) = 1. The red-highlighted curves show correlations similar to those shown in Fig. 1, but that extend to frequencies where the signal is much stronger. The average over all simulations reveals the bias in hat{H}_text{ETFE}(omega_l): in Fig. 2(a), Re{hat{H}_text{ETFE}} falls from the correct value of 1 to the biased value of 0 as the frequency increases above 5 THz, where the signal reaches the noise floor in Fig. 1(b). Also, the noise distribution for hat{H}_text{ETFE}(omega_l) develops wide, non-Gaussian tails at frequencies where the signal is small, because noise fluctuation that cause |X(omega_l)|rightarrow 0 become more likely, and these in turn cause hat{H}_text{ETFE}(omega_l) to diverge. If the noise is uncorrelated, such large fluctuations are only expected when the signal is small. But in the more typical case that the noise is correlated, they can influence frequencies well within the primary signal bandwidth, as indicated by the red-highlighted curves in Figs. 2(c) and 2(d). In short, while an ETFE fit with Eq. An alternative approach is to use a least-squares (LS) procedure that minimizes the sum of the squared differences between the output signal and the transformed input signal, begin{aligned} Q_text{LS}(ensuremath{bm{theta}}) &= sum_{l=0}^{N-1}left|Y(omega_l) - H(omega_l;ensuremath{bm{theta}})X(omega_l)right|^2 = sum_{k=0}^{N-1}left{y(t_k) - [h(ensuremath{bm{theta}})ensuremath{circledast}x](t_k)right}^2. label{eq:LS-cost}end{aligned} Here, h(t_k; ensuremath{bm{theta}}) is the model impulse response, equal to the inverse DFT of H(omega_l;ensuremath{bm{theta}}), and [h(ensuremath{bm{theta}})ensuremath{circledast}x](t_k) represents the convolution of impulse response with the input vector at the time t_k. The equivalence between the frequency-domain sum and the time-domain sum is assured by Parseval’s theorem. The LS procedure avoids the division by X(omega_l) that distorts the noise distribution of the ETFE in the small-signal limit, and the time-domain residuals r_text{LS}(t_k; ensuremath{bm{hat{theta}}}) = y(t_k) - [h(ensuremath{bm{theta}})ensuremath{circledast}x](t_k) are a sensitive indicator of fit quality. But the uniform weighting in Eq. ([eq:LS-cost]) implicitly assumes that this noise is constant in time, which as we have noted is frequently not the case. In principle, we should be able to account for time-dependent noise by assigning appropriate weights to the sum, but this is not as simple as it might seem. The problem is that Eq. ([eq:LS-cost]) treats the input and output noise asymmetrically, which we can see more clearly if we express it explicitly in terms of the time-domain signal and noise sequences: Q_text{LS}(ensuremath{bm{theta}}) = sum_{k=0}^{N-1}left[psi(t_k) - [h(ensuremath{bm{theta}})ensuremath{circledast}mu](t_k) + sigma_psi(t_k) - [h(ensuremath{bm{theta}})ensuremath{circledast}(sigma_muepsilon)](t_k)right]^2. label{eq:LS-cost-noise} This asymmetry results from the implicit assumption that all noise is restricted to the output variable in an LS fit, so that the term [h(ensuremath{bm{theta}})ensuremath{circledast}(sigma_muepsilon)](t_k) can be ignored. When input noise is present—as it always is in THz-TDS—the optimal fit weights will depend not just on the measurement noise sequences but also on h(t_k; ensuremath{bm{theta}}). Any modification of Eq. ([eq:LS-cost]) with constant weights will generically bias the parameter estimates toward values that minimize the input noise contribution, and will cause the GOF statistic, S_text{LS} = Q_text{LS}(ensuremath{bm{hat{theta}}}), to have a distribution that also depends on h(t_k; ensuremath{bm{theta}}). As we discuss below, these problems can all be solved with a fit procedure derived from the maximum-likelihood principle. Maximum-likelihood estimation of a parameterized transfer function model The likelihood function is equivalent to the probability density for obtaining the measured data under the assumptions of a given model, and forms the theoretical basis for the standard least-squares fit procedure. To define it in the THz-TDS context, we express the measured input and output signals in vector notation as ensuremath{bm{x}}= [x(t_0), x(t_1), ldots, x(t_{N-1})]^ensuremath{mathsf{T}} and ensuremath{bm{y}}= [y(t_0), y(t_1), ldots, y(t_{N-1})]^ensuremath{mathsf{T}}, respectively, and assume that they represent noisy measurements of bandlimited but otherwise unknown ideal signal vectors ensuremath{bm{mu}}= [mu(t_0), mu(t_1), ldots, mu(t_{N-1})]^ensuremath{mathsf{T}} and ensuremath{bm{psi}}= [psi(t_0), psi(t_1), ldots, psi(t_{N-1})]^ensuremath{mathsf{T}} with noise covariance matrices ensuremath{bm{Sigma_x}} and ensuremath{bm{Sigma_y}}, respectively. We further assume that ensuremath{bm{mu}} and ensuremath{bm{psi}} satisfy the linear constraint ensuremath{bm{psi}}= ensuremath{bm{h}}(ensuremath{bm{theta}})ensuremath{bm{mu}}, where ensuremath{bm{h}} is a known matrix function of an unknown p-dimensional parameter vector ensuremath{bm{theta}}. The likelihood function is then a product of two multivariate Gaussian distributions, L(ensuremath{bm{theta}}, ensuremath{bm{mu}}, ensuremath{bm{psi}}; ensuremath{bm{x}}, ensuremath{bm{y}}) = frac{exp[-frac{1}{2}(ensuremath{bm{x}}- ensuremath{bm{mu}})^ensuremath{mathsf{T}},ensuremath{bm{Sigma_x}}^{-1}(ensuremath{bm{x}}-ensuremath{bm{mu}})]}{sqrt{(2pi)^Ndet(ensuremath{bm{Sigma_x}})}}frac{exp[-frac{1}{2}(ensuremath{bm{y}}- ensuremath{bm{psi}})^ensuremath{mathsf{T}},ensuremath{bm{Sigma_y}}^{-1}(ensuremath{bm{y}}-ensuremath{bm{psi}})]}{sqrt{(2pi)^Ndet(ensuremath{bm{Sigma_y}})}}, label{eq:likelihood-tf} where the dependence on ensuremath{bm{theta}} on the right side of the expression is implicit in the constraint between ensuremath{bm{mu}} and ensuremath{bm{psi}}. The signal vectors ensuremath{bm{mu}} and ensuremath{bm{psi}} appear as arguments in the likelihood function but are otherwise unimportant—the statistical literature aptly describes these as nuisance parameters, which we must eliminate to estimate ensuremath{bm{theta}}, the parameters of interest. We can use discrete-time Fourier analysis to obtain explicit expressions for ensuremath{bm{h}}(ensuremath{bm{theta}}), ensuremath{bm{Sigma_x}}, and ensuremath{bm{Sigma_y}}. The time-domain transfer matrix ensuremath{bm{h}}(ensuremath{bm{theta}}) is h_{jk}(ensuremath{bm{theta}}) = frac{1}{N}sum_{l = -N/2 + 1}^{N/2-1} H(omega_l; ensuremath{bm{theta}}) exp[-2pi i(j - k)l/N] + frac{1}{N}Re[H(omega_{N/2};ensuremath{bm{theta}})]cos[pi(j-k)], label{eq:transfer-matrix} where omega_l now extends to negative values of l, and N is assumed even; for odd N, the sum runs from l = -(N-1)/2 to (N-1)/2 and the anomalous term at the Nyquist frequency is absent. Similarly, we can define the discrete-time matrix representation of the time-derivative operator, ensuremath{bm{D}}, as D_{jk} = frac{1}{N}sum_{l = -lfloor (N-1)/2 rfloor}^{lfloor (N-1)/2 rfloor} -iomega_l exp[-2pi i(j - k)l/N], label{eq:D-matrix} by recognizing that the transfer function for the continuous time-derivative operator is H(omega) = -iomega. Note that Eq. ([eq:D-matrix]) is valid for all values of N because Re[-iomega_{N/2}]=0, so the anomalous term in Eq. ([eq:transfer-matrix]) is zero. Equation ([eq:D-matrix]) allows us to express the noise variance function sigma_mu^2(t) in Eq. ([eq:noise-amplitude]) as a matrix function in discrete time, left[ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{mu}})right]_{jk} = delta_{jk}left[ensuremath{sigma_alpha}^2 + ensuremath{sigma_beta}^2mu_{k}^2 + ensuremath{sigma_tau}^2(ensuremath{bm{D}}ensuremath{bm{mu}})_{k}^2right]. label{eq:V-matrix} The covariance matrices for ensuremath{bm{x}} and ensuremath{bm{y}} are then ensuremath{bm{Sigma_x}}= ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{mu}}), quadensuremath{bm{Sigma_y}}= ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{psi}}). label{eq:Sigma-xy} The maximum-likelihood (ML) estimate for the model parameters is given by the vectors ensuremath{bm{hat{mu}}}, ensuremath{bm{hat{psi}}}, and ensuremath{bm{hat{theta}}} that maximize L subject to the constraint ensuremath{bm{psi}}= ensuremath{bm{h}}(ensuremath{bm{theta}})ensuremath{bm{mu}}. Alternatively, we can minimize the negative-log likelihood, begin{split} -ln L(ensuremath{bm{theta}}, ensuremath{bm{mu}}, ensuremath{bm{psi}}; ensuremath{bm{x}}, ensuremath{bm{y}}) &= Nln(2pi) + frac{1}{2}ln{det[ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{mu}})]} + frac{1}{2}ln{det[ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{psi}})]} &quad+ frac{1}{2}left{(ensuremath{bm{x}}- ensuremath{bm{mu}})^ensuremath{mathsf{T}},left[ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{mu}})right]^{-1}(ensuremath{bm{x}}- ensuremath{bm{mu}}) + (ensuremath{bm{y}}- ensuremath{bm{psi}})^ensuremath{mathsf{T}},left[ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{psi}})right]^{-1}(ensuremath{bm{y}}- ensuremath{bm{psi}})right}, end{split} label{eq:NLL} also subject to the constraint, where the last term now has the familiar least-squares form. The dependence of the covariance matrices on the signal vectors prevents us from using a standard least-squares optimization algorithm to minimize Eq. ([eq:NLL]), but to a very good approximation we can substitute ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{mu}}) approx ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{x}}) and ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{psi}}) approx ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{y}}) to obtain begin{split} -ln L(ensuremath{bm{theta}}, ensuremath{bm{mu}}, ensuremath{bm{psi}}; ensuremath{bm{x}}, ensuremath{bm{y}}) &approx Nln(2pi) + frac{1}{2}ln{det[ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{x}})]} + frac{1}{2}ln{det[ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{y}})]} &quad+ frac{1}{2}left{(ensuremath{bm{x}}- ensuremath{bm{mu}})^ensuremath{mathsf{T}},left[ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{x}})right]^{-1}(ensuremath{bm{x}}- ensuremath{bm{mu}}) + (ensuremath{bm{y}}- ensuremath{bm{psi}})^ensuremath{mathsf{T}},left[ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{y}})right]^{-1}(ensuremath{bm{y}}- ensuremath{bm{psi}})right}. end{split} label{eq:NLL-approximate} The first three terms are now all constant, so we can focus on minimizing the last term, which we multiply by two to obtain a cost function in what is known as a total least-squares form, tilde{Q}_text{TLS}(ensuremath{bm{theta}}, ensuremath{bm{mu}}, ensuremath{bm{psi}}) = (ensuremath{bm{x}}- ensuremath{bm{mu}})^ensuremath{mathsf{T}},[ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{x}})]^{-1}(ensuremath{bm{x}}- ensuremath{bm{mu}}) + (ensuremath{bm{y}}- ensuremath{bm{psi}})^ensuremath{mathsf{T}},[ensuremath{bm{V}}(ensuremath{bm{y}})]^{-1}(ensuremath{bm{y}}- ensuremath{bm{psi}}), label{eq:TLS-cost-tilde} which is also subject to the constraint ensuremath{bm{psi}}= ensuremath{bm{h}}(ensuremath{bm{theta}})ensuremath{bm{mu}}. Note that the total least-squares cost function treats the input and output variables symmetrically, unlike the conventional least-squares cost function in Eq. ([eq:LS-cost]). Maximum-likelihood estimation of the noise model We have assumed so far that the noise amplitudes sigma_alpha, sigma_beta, and sigma_tau in Eq. ([eq:V-matrix]) are known, but in general they must also be determined experimentally. One way to do this is to measure the noise at three different points on the THz waveform: as we saw in Fig. 1, the sigma_beta term dominates near the peak, the sigma_tau term dominates where the signal crosses zero with a large slope, and the sigma_alpha term dominates where both the signal and its slope are small. Another approach is to determine the variance as a function of time from a set of repeated waveform measurements, then obtain the noise parameters from a fit with Eq. ([eq:noise-amplitude]). But both of these methods are susceptible to systematic errors from drift, which produces excess signal variability over the timescale of multiple measurements. In this section, we describe a likelihood method for estimating the noise parameters that accounts for this drift. We consider repeated measurements of an ideal primary waveform, mu(t), which has an amplitude and a delay that drift on a timescale longer than the waveform acquisition time. We can then associate each measurement with an ideal secondary waveform, zeta(t; A_l, eta_l) = A_lmu(t - eta_l), label{eq:zeta} where A_l is the relative amplitude, eta_l is the delay, and l in {0, 1, ldots, M-1}. We also set A_0 = 1 and eta_0 = 0, so that zeta(t; A_0, eta_0) = mu(t). As before, we sample these waveforms at the nominal times t_k = kT, k in {0, 1,ldots, N-1} to obtain the ideal primary waveform vector ensuremath{bm{mu}}= [mu_0, mu_1, ldots, mu_{N-1}]^ensuremath{mathsf{T}} and M measurement vectors ensuremath{bm{x}}_l = [x_l(t_0), x_l(t_1), ldots, x_l(t_{N-1})]^ensuremath{mathsf{T}}, which we can arrange in an Ntimes M matrix ensuremath{bm{X}}= [ensuremath{bm{x}}_0, ensuremath{bm{x}}_1, ldots, ensuremath{bm{x}}_{N-1}]. We also write the amplitudes and delays in vector form, ensuremath{bm{A}}= [A_0, A_1, ldots, A_{M-1}]^ensuremath{mathsf{T}} and ensuremath{bm{eta}}= [eta_0, eta_1, ldots, eta_{M-1}]^ensuremath{mathsf{T}}, respectively. For example, in 1000 Monte Carlo simulations of M=10 repeated measurements using the waveforms described in Sec. 2, the ratios of the ML estimates to their true values are ensuremath{hat{sigma}_alpha}/ensuremath{sigma_alpha}= 0.95 pm 0.02, ensuremath{hat{sigma}_beta}/ensuremath{sigma_beta}= 0.94 pm 0.03, and ensuremath{hat{sigma}_tau}/ensuremath{sigma_tau}= 0.89 pm 0.09, all significantly below unity. Increasing the number of observations to M=50 reduces the bias to ensuremath{hat{sigma}_alpha}/ensuremath{sigma_alpha}= 0.990 pm 0.007, ensuremath{hat{sigma}_beta}/ensuremath{sigma_beta}= 0.98 pm 0.01, and ensuremath{hat{sigma}_tau}/ensuremath{sigma_tau}= 0.93 pm 0.04, but some bias remains in ensuremath{hat{sigma}_beta} and ensuremath{hat{sigma}_tau} even in the limit Mrightarrowinfty. This residual bias arises because the number of elements in ensuremath{bm{A}} and ensuremath{bm{eta}} both grow with the number of observations, which allows us to account for drift but dilutes some of the information that the data provide about the noise. In principle, we can resolve this problem by integrating out all of the nuisance parameters in Eq. ([eq:likelihood-noise]) to obtain a marginal likelihood that depends only on ensuremath{sigma_alpha}, ensuremath{sigma_beta}, and ensuremath{sigma_tau}, but this involves computationally expensive integrations for a relatively small benefit. As we discuss below, we can remove much of the bias by simply rescaling the noise parameters by a suitable correction factor. To determine the bias correction factor it is helpful to consider a simplified example in which there is no drift and only additive noise, so that A_l = 1 and eta_l = 0 for all l and ensuremath{sigma_beta}= ensuremath{sigma_tau}= 0. To remove this bias we can apply Bessel’s correction, which replaces the factor of 1/M with 1/(M-1) in Eq. ([eq:samplevar]). Alternatively, we can multiply ensuremath{hat{sigma}_alpha}^2 by M/(M-1) in Eq. ([eq:ML-est-simple]). Returning to Eq. ([eq:ML-cost]) and following similar reasoning, we can justify rescaling all of the ML noise parameter estimates by the same factor, begin{aligned} ensuremath{hat{sigma}_alpha}^* &= ensuremath{hat{sigma}_alpha}sqrt{frac{M}{M-1}}, & ensuremath{hat{sigma}_beta}^* &= ensuremath{hat{sigma}_beta}sqrt{frac{M}{M-1}}, & ensuremath{hat{sigma}_tau}^* &= ensuremath{hat{sigma}_tau}sqrt{frac{M}{M-1}}. label{eq:unbiasedsigma}end{aligned} With these corrections, the Monte Carlo simulations with M=10 yield ensuremath{hat{sigma}_alpha}^*/ensuremath{sigma_alpha}= 1.00 pm 0.02, ensuremath{hat{sigma}_beta}^*/ensuremath{sigma_beta}= 0.99 pm 0.03, and ensuremath{hat{sigma}_tau}^*/ensuremath{sigma_tau}= 0.94 pm 0.09, which are all within the statistical uncertainty of the true values. The simulations with M=50 yield lower statistical uncertainty, but with the same average values: ensuremath{hat{sigma}_alpha}^*/ensuremath{sigma_alpha}= 1.000 pm 0.007, ensuremath{hat{sigma}_beta}^*/ensuremath{sigma_beta}= 0.99 pm 0.01, and ensuremath{hat{sigma}_tau}^*/ensuremath{sigma_tau}= 0.94 pm 0.04. For all practical purposes, the remaining bias in ensuremath{hat{sigma}_beta}^* and ensuremath{hat{sigma}_tau}^* is negligible.
